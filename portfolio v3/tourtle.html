Meet Tourtle

A brand new way for kids to experience trips to the museum. Tourtle is the ultimate companion, helping kids of all ages engage more with Museum material while having fun at the same time.

How does Tourtle Work

Tourtle was designed with simplicity in mind, and has 4 easy steps to follow:

1. Attach Tourtle to the kids wrist or Arm

2. Touch Tourtle against any museum information plaque

3. Hold Tourtle up to your ear and enjoy the story about the exhibit

4. Parents can see the visited exhibits and pull up quizzes and discussions

Why Tourtle

Traditional museums tend to focus on looking and reading, you walk up to and exhibit, you look at it and you read the information plaque and then you move on to the next exhibit. The problem is, kids don't truly engage with the museum material because it's not presented to them in a form they enjoy. Kids crave interactivity I need a way to digest the information in a simpler manner, this is why Tourtle was created.

Research Process

Before Tourtle what is created, we needed to examine the traditional museum experience and see if we could find opportunities to improve it. To do this, we employed several research methodologies to help gain a complete picture of the traditional museum experience.

Literature Reviews

Museums have been around for thousands of years, and there's a lot of great knowledge which we could take advantage of that helps describe the museum experience while also pointing out potential issues. To take advantage of all this information, we conducted literature reviews to specifically look at how the museum experience works for children. Through conducting a literature review, we were able to find great background information, which can be seen through several of these quotes:

“What children take away from their experiences is determined by a combination of what they do and what they talk about.”

"The combination of hands‐on engagement with objects and elaborative talk during events may promote successful transfer"

"Overall, conversation and building tips helped parents to initiate conversations with children during an interactive activity."

Observations

In addition to literature reviews we felt it was crucial to gain first-hand real-world experience with the potential issues that kids have with the museum experience; to do this we decided to visit two different museums in the Detroit area and act as silent observers to be as unobtrusive as possible.

While visiting each museum, we observed that kids are not engaging with traditional museum exhibits; they were much more interested in exhibits that allowed them to interact via touching, audio, or creating. Additionally, we also learned parents are trying to engage with their kids while and help them learn about the exhibits.

Surveys

Finally, we created and deployed a 26 question survey which was aimed at learning more about people's museum experience. In particular, we wanted to focus on how going to the museum with a child affected their experience, and what their point of view was regarding how to improve the experience for kids.

“They (the kids) were really excited and asked a lot of questions.”

"We asked them questions, and what hey thought about what they saw sometimes, lots of times we let them explore to their interest."

"The more interactive, the better. Also having information aimed at multiple levels so kids of different ages through adults can all get something out of the exhibit."

Design Process

After conducting all of our research methodologies, we moved onto the design phase of the project, which consistd of (insert number of steps here) steps.

Ideation

We didn't want to become fixated on any one particular solution at this point, so out first step was creating an 8 x 10 matrix table with the sole purpose of generating as many ideas as possible. Using this method we were able to generate 80 unique ideas which all had the potential to solve our design problem.

Narrowing the Selection:

We narrowed our final selection down to 3 potential choices based on 3 select criteria: How well do they solve the problem? How feasible is this solutions? Would our target audience be interested in using this solution?

A toy kids can carry around that tells them stories about museum exhibits

An explorer kit kids can use to act like professionals and engage with materials as "experts"

A 3D printing exhibit that gives kids a souvenir and a story

Prototyping - 1st Try

For our first prototype, we used an electronics kit called LittleBits, and paired it with a toy bear stuffed animal. By using LittleBits, we were able to create a solution that allowed us to play audio through our phones by pushing a button on the bears back pack.

Prototyping - Feedback

After creating our prototype, we wanted to test it out with users and other UX professionals to get their opinion on it. As was expected, our prototype had a few major concerns that needed to be addressed:

  • The bear as a whole was too large, children couldn't carry it around easily
  • Because of the internals, the bear became too heavy to carry around and fatigued children easily
  • There was concern about the bear being left behind as kids run throughout the museum
  • LittleBits was too limited in its implementation and couldn't effectively communicate the product

Prototyping - Final Interation

After receieving feedback on our first prototype, we made many changes. We switched our stuffed animal to a much smaller slap-bracelet, which meant less weight and bulk to carry around. Using a slap bracelet also means the toy will always be attached to the kid, making it less of a burden on them. Secondly, we changed the technical implementation; we swapped out the LittleBits system in favor of an Arduino because of the added control and flexibility to the project.

Limitations

Our second prototype ended up having a few issues with its implementation, the biggest being that we couldn't actually integrate the Arduino system into the prototype. After working on it for several weeks, we were unable to find a way to shrink the prototype down to a small enough size to fit into the turtles shell, so we ended up leaving them as separate components in the end.
