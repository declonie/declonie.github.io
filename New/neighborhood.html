Neighborhood Center Check In

Creating a simple, user friendly check-in kiosk with a focus on capturing quality data from guests and speeding up check-in

Problem Statement

The design team was approached to help improve the check in experience for a brand new endeavor the company was venturing into, community centers. The current check in process is outdated and was not properly thought through during the centers creation; they wanted us to do a full suite of both research and design to help improve this process. Additionally, we were asked to ensure our solution was scalable, efficient, easy to use, and consumer friendly.

Kiosk Features

Keycard Scanning for Quick Sign In

In order to make the kiosk as easy to use as possible, we created a keycard which is given to guests upon their first visit to the center. This provides an easy way for returning guests to instantly identify themselves on the kiosk, and allows for quick check-in.

Progressive Data Collection

As guests continue to visit the center, the system will gradually open up and ask for additional information. We purposely spread the data collection aspect out to give guests a chance to warm up to the center and staff, before asking them for personal information.

Easy Enrollment in Classes & Activities

The kiosk lists out all current classes and activies going on for each day of the week, allowing guests to easily browse current and upcoming classes. With the press of a button then can instantly register as long as spots are available.

Contextual Inquiry - Deepdive

The main goal of our contextual inquiry was to gain a better understanding of how the current center's check in works. We spent an entire week at the center, making sure that we had enough time to fully observe guests, staff members, classes and activites, and of course our main focus, the check in process. We made sure to be present right as various types of classes and activities took place since this is when the current check in process was tested the most. Classes and activities are also when guests and staff members tend to interact and we wanted to see what their general interactions were, and if they had any effect on the check-in process.

Contextual Inquiry Findings

Concierge is the Only Way to Check In

Guests who visit the center must go through the concierge. With no alternative methods, its possible for long lines to build up.

New Guests Have a Harder Time Checking In

When a new guest visits the center, they need to provide more information than returning guests, making the overall check in process take longer.

Staff and Guests Have Strong Relationships

Staff members have formed deep personal relationships with many of the guests who visit the center. This relationship drives guests to keep coming back.

Long Lines Build Up Before Classes

Guests tend to flood in 5 to 10 minutes before a class starts, and when multiple guests arrive at once, the check in process becomes a bottle-neck.

User Interviews - Deep Dive

Lastly we conducted user interviews to get a general idea of how both the staff and guests felt about the neighborhood center; in particular we structured questions around what currently works well, what's frustrating, and what do both sides feel could be improved. we also had a large emphasis on what brings guests into the center; this was to help gather data about the types of classes and activities which are most prevalent.

Design Principles

Before we began actually designing, we created two separate sets of design principles based on our core user groups, staff and guests. We outlined the most important needs for each group based on the research findings. During the prototype creation phase, we constantly referenced the design principles to ensure we were staying focused on the most important needs for each user group.

Staff Design Principles

Accurate

Any design needs to allow for correctness and precision the first around

Clear

Any tasks done need to be easy to understand and intuitive

Efficient

Checking-in should be a simple, straightforward process

Guest Design Principles

Progressive Data Collection

Treat data collection like getting to know someone, build trust and conversations

Private

Give guests privacy when checking-in, make them feel safe as they provide their information

Transparent

Tell guests why data is being collected and how it will be used

Personable

Try to retain a human touch as much as possible through friendly and conversational tone

Tasks & Flow

Before prototyping, we needed to lay out all of the various tasks users need to accomplish using our kiosk. We hosted a whiteboarding session with designers, researchers, and stakeholders to go over the tasks we could think of, and also gain additional input from other parties to see if there was anything we missed. After these sessions were held, we strung everything together into a flow diagram to determine what we would need to show in our prototype. A version of the flow diagram can be found below.

Prototyping

We wanted to simulate a genuine check-in experience as best we could, including letting users have free control to input all of the information that they would normally put in during a check in process. This would help us get a realistic sense of how long these tasks would take for the average user, while also providing great feedback about their overall experience using our proposed solution.

To create our prototype we relied on using Axure, which is a tool we typically don't use due to its complexity and steep learning curve, but once the program is learned it offers a much wider array of interactions than any other prototyping software on the market.

User Testing & Feedback

After creating our prototype, it was time to put it in front of users to gauge their opinion of our proposed solution. We created a variety of tasks for users to do such as:

  • Registering a new account
  • Finding and enrolling in a class/activity
  • Using a keycard to check in

We tested these tasks with 6 different users, and received the following feedback:

User Testing Feedback

Font Sizes are Too Small

We used a standard 14pt font minmum for all screens, but many of our elderly users felt it was too small

Keycard is a Great Feature

Users loved the ability to scan a keycard in order to check in for events instantly

Assitance is Sometimes Needed

Our guests weren't always able to succeed in their tasks and wanted to make sure staff members could help them when they struggle

Iteration

After synthesizing all of the feedback we received, we went back to our prototype and made a few changes to our system. We had two major concerns that we wanted to address, they were:

  • Increasing the font size to improve readability and usability
  • Adding a way for users to get help when they are struggling

To address these issues we changed/added the following:

Added an "I need assistance" link to every page, which immediately alerts any available staff

Increased the minumum font size on all screens from 14 pixels to 18 pixels. (This is hard to show)
