Interactive Voice Response (IVR) System Re-Design

Project Description:

All business with customer service lines use a phone tree system of menus and options to help guide the caller to a specialist who can help them resolve their issues. For this project, we were tasked with examining the current IVR system in place, and trying to find ways to help improve it through research, design, and heuristics. In particular, the business wanted us to focus on making the system easier to use since a large portion of the caller demographic are adults over the age of 50.

Methodologies Used

IVR Flow Diagram Mapping

Mapping out the current IVR to learn its structure, flow, and possible interactions

Heuristic Evaluation

Using a set of fundamental principles to uncover issues which may affect the caller experience

Usability Testing

Sitting down with users and learning how they interact with IVRs and where potential improvements can be made

Journey Mapping

Looking at and calling our competitors to see how they've structured their IVR systems

Final Recommendations

Step 2: Heuristic Evaluation

Like any other system, IVR's have core design principles that should be upheld for the best possible experience and usability. Sources such as Google Scholar helped us find articles showing the limits of human patience, ability to memorize and understand options, and mental fatigue. Industry leaders, such as Twilio, Helped break down simple best practices to follow when it comes to designing an IVR.

Heuristic Evaluation Criteria

Message Length

A user should not have to wait more than 30 seconds for a prompt to finish

Menu Options

A user should never have more than 5 options to choose from at once

Live Agent Option

A user should be able to dial out to a live user at any time to help resolve their inquiry

User Inputs Should Transfer

A user should have any inputs passed to the representative so they don't need to enter it again

Allow Barge In

A user should be able to interrupt the system at any point; this is expecially valuable for repeat callers

Offering Multiple Languages

A user should be able to select their preferred language from the beginning of a call

Allow Multiple Data Entry Attempts

A user should be able to ttempt entering data multiple times before erroring out

Follow Standard Key Press Patterns

A user should be able to follow DTMF conventions to navigate the IVR system

Call Transfer Prompts

Prompts related to call transfers should only be heard immediately before the transfer happens

Instructions Before Inputs

A user should be able to hear any instructions before the key to execute them

Leave Marketing Material Out

A user should be able to accomplish their tasks without being bogged down by marketing

Professional Voice and Tone

A user should feel like the system has a professional and friendly tone that reflects the brand

Heuristic Evaluation Findings

Not Following Key Press Standards

Our IVR system does not follow current key press patterns, and worse, often re-assigns what keys do as users navigate through the system

Prompts Are Too Long

Our IVR system has several prompts that exceed 30 seconds which goes against the recommended length of 30 seconds or less

Too Many Menu Choices

Our IVR system often has menus that exceed 5 choices, this makes it harder for users to remember choices and causes them to repeat selections

Marketing Material Clutter

Our IVR system presents marketing material before our callers needs are resolved

Poor Voice and Tone

Our IVR system uses very stiff, unfriendly language, which can make users feel like the company doesn't care about them or their issues

Step 3: Creating Prototypes for Testing

After conducting the heuristic review and seeing where our current IVR system falls short, we turned out attention to creating new workflows which could be used for user testing sessions. These flows were built upon the lessons we learned from the heuristic exercise to create an experience that follows best practices and makes it easier for our users to achieve their goals.

Flow Breakdows

Flow 1 - The Current System

Flow Features:

  1. The exact experience our current users experience
  2. All current heuristic violations were preserved
  3. All messaging, inputs, and pauses were also preserved

Why did we test this flow? We wanted to gain a users perspective on the current system and the struggles they may face. Testing the original also creates a stronger argument for implementing changes if the results that come back aren't positive.

Flow 2 - The Middle Ground

Flow Features:

  1. Trimmed down messaging to meet heuristic time standards
  2. Changed inputs to meet heuristic input standards
  3. Revised error messaging to help users recover easily

Why did we test this flow? We wanted to create a middle ground flow that passes all of the heuristics which we covered, without being such a radical change that the business would immediately reject it due to having too many changes.

Flow 3 - The Drastic Overhaul

Flow Features:

  1. A complete revamp of the menu's users will encounter to meet heuritsic standards
  2. Automation injected into the experience to lessen the amount of information users need to provide
  3. Drastically overhauled messaging to create a friendlier more human-centered experience

Why did we test this flow? Drastically changing our current IVR allowed the business see the benefits of an IVR system that is solely focused on creating a simple and easy to use experience, without any of the issues of the current system present.

Step 4: Usability Testing - Building an Actual IVR

After creating new flows for our IVR system, we needed to test them to ensure they were easy to use, quick, and allowed our users to accomplish their various goals. In order to test out each flow, we built a makeshift IVR system using Twilio. Twilio allowed us to create an extensive IVR, complete with error scenarios, key presses, pauses, various voices and tones, and most importantly: we could program logic into the system which would actually let our users simulate tasks.

Testing Goals:

Ensure users can authenticate themselves in the IVR system to reach the self-service menu

Ensure users can accomplish tasks once they have been authenticated

Discern spoken voice, tone, and speed preferences for our users

Evaluate whether the revised language and trimmed content still provide a usable system

Determine what tasks users are comfortable accomplishing through an IVR

Usability Testing Findings

Less Choices is Better

Stripping the IVR self-service menu down to 5 choices made it easier for users to accomplish their goals

Member Dedicated IVR Channel

Don't combine IVR's for providers, suppliers, and members into one. Regular users find it too difficult to differentiate themselves.

Voice and Tone Matters

Having a clear, professional, and empathetic voice through the system makes it easier to use and can affect how users perceive the company

Simple Language is Better

Users don't speak or think like insurance companies; we need to use terminology they undertand and can relate to

Don't Force IVR Usage

Most people don't want to use IVR systems to accomplish a lot of their tasks; focus on simple tasks they can easily accomplish with minimal inputs.

Step 5: IVR Journey Mapping

To get an accurate picture of our callers experience when using the IVR system, we created a journey map to show the steps involved in a call and the overall mood of our callers during each step. We noticed that most callers grew more frustrated the longer they spent in the IVR system, and only felt better once they were eventually connected to an actual human being after either requesting it or erroring out in the call tree.
